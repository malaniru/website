1. AI Market Trends

Monday reopens the equities story that sat frozen over a closed weekend, and the two loudest threads — one internal to the Claude stack, one in the chip complex — both moved in ways that revise, rather than confirm, last week's expectations.

First, the Fable 5 timeline slipped again. What the prior two briefings framed as a hard July 12 cutoff — with usage-credit pricing kicking in today — has been deferred a second time. Included access on paid plans now runs through July 19, and the metered-credit switch ($10 per million input tokens, $50 per million output, the highest pricing Anthropic has published for a GA model) moves to July 20; the 50%-higher Claude Code weekly rate limits ride along to the same date (Source: Forbes, July 13; BleepingComputer, July 2026 — press-reported). This is the second extension in a week (July 7 → July 12 → July 19), and the pattern itself is the signal: the constraint is capacity, not policy, and the date is soft enough that planning around any single one of these deadlines has repeatedly proven premature.

Second, the SK Hynix listing entered its "morning after" phase. The record US debut — a ~13% first-day pop Friday, July 10 to close near $168.01 off a $149 ADR pricing, a raise of roughly $26.5 billion billed as the largest-ever US first-time share sale by a foreign company — is now colliding with a sharp reaction in the home market: SK Hynix's Seoul-listed shares (000660.KS) fell a record ~15% Monday, with traders citing worries the AI-memory boom has become overstretched and a rotation into the newly listed US ADRs (Source: Bloomberg, "SK Hynix Shares Drop in Seoul After Much-Hyped US Trading Debut," July 13; CNBC, July 10 — press-reported). Two mechanical events land today regardless of price: the US ADR ticker changes from the when-issued SKHYV to the regular-way SKHY, and GraniteShares' leveraged ETFs SKUU (2x long) and SKDD (-2x short) begin trading (Source: Nasdaq Trader, DTN #2026-11; GraniteShares, July 13 — primary/vendor). Detail in Section 5.

Third, the surrounding semiconductor complex is still digesting the early-July valuation-and-sentiment reset rather than a demand collapse. Coverage continues to tie the rout to a report that SK Hynix plans to slow high-bandwidth-memory (HBM) capacity expansion and tilt toward lower-cost commodity DRAM, with Micron, Intel, and AMD taking the sharpest single-name hits and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) off several percent on the move (Source: Intellectia.ai, "AI Chip Stocks Plunge," July 2026; Forbes, July 8 — press-reported; figures vary by outlet and window). Nvidia has continued to trade apart from the group, holding up better than the sector through the selloff (Source: 24/7 Wall St., July 9; GuruFocus, July — press-reported). The Seoul drop feeds directly into that narrative: the very supplier whose record listing anchored the week is also the name at the center of the HBM-discipline worry.

Fourth, the protocol and enterprise-enablement layer keeps standardizing underneath all of it. The MCP 2026-07-28 specification remains on track — a release candidate is available now, with the final spec and its stateless-first core landing July 28 (more in Section 3). Microsoft's 60+-server MCP catalog across Copilot, Foundry, and GitHub from earlier in the month underscores how quickly the connector surface is consolidating (Source: Model Context Protocol blog; Microsoft Power Platform blog, July 6 — primary/vendor). The throughline is unchanged: capital and attention are concentrating on the operational and governance layer, meaning deployment, connectors, and agent reliability, as much as on the models themselves.

MCP 2026-07-28 spec release candidate (carry). The largest revision since launch is available as a release candidate, with the final spec landing July 28. The headline change is a stateless protocol core — the Mcp-Session-Id header and protocol-level session are removed, so any request can land on any server instance and horizontal deployments no longer need sticky routing or shared session stores at the protocol layer (state moves to explicit, model-passed handles). It also adds MCP Apps (server-rendered UIs), a Tasks extension for long-running work, OAuth/OIDC-aligned authorization, and a formal deprecation policy. The ten-week window is for SDK maintainers and client implementers to validate against real workloads; Tier-1 SDKs are expected to ship support within it (Source: Model Context Protocol blog, "The 2026-07-28 MCP Specification Release Candidate" — primary).

Gemini 3.5 Pro — still in preview (carry). GA remains a July target in limited Vertex AI enterprise preview; the loudest rumor is a July 17 date with a 2M context window and stronger math/coding, but Google has published no confirmed general-availability date, model card, or pricing. Reports attribute the slip to reasoning, coding, and token-efficiency refinements after early testing: treat the July 17 date and all spec claims as unconfirmed (Source: BigGo Finance; Coursiv; Geeky Gadgets — press/rumor; unconfirmed).

GPT-5.6 general availability (carry). Roughly four days into GA across ChatGPT, ChatGPT Work, Codex, and the API, with API pricing at Sol $5/$30, Terra $2.50/$15, Luna $1/$6 per million tokens and a vendor 54% coding-token-efficiency claim for Sol (unaudited).
