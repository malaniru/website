The Current State of AI Adoption in Philanthropy: Tools Without Discipline

Executive Summary

Philanthropy has crossed the experimentation threshold with AI and stalled there. Across the two best sector-representative surveys available — the Technology Association of Grantmakers’ State of Philanthropy Tech and the Center for Effective Philanthropy’s AI With Purpose — the same shape appears: roughly four in five grantmaking organizations now touch AI in some form, yet only a tiny fraction have moved it past individual experimentation into governed, organization-wide practice (TAG 2024; CEP 2025). The gap between “someone on staff is using ChatGPT” and “we have a policy, a training program, and a compliance posture” is the gap most organizations now have to close — and right now, for most, it is close to wide open.

This report does three things. First, it maps the real adoption picture on both sides of the funding relationship — the foundations writing checks and the nonprofits cashing them — because AI is dissolving the traditional wall between them. Second, it documents who is using which tools and for what purposes, from enterprise grants-management platforms adding AI features to the marquee AI-for-good funders deploying nine-figure portfolios. Third, and most important if you are the one adopting AI, it isolates the specific gaps — in policy, training, and compliance — that separate safe, productive AI use from risky experimentation, and shows where to focus first.

The headline is uncomfortable but clarifying: the philanthropic sector has adopted the tools of AI far faster than it has adopted the governance, literacy, and accountability that make those tools safe. Nearly 90 percent of foundations provide no AI support to their grantees; roughly two-thirds of organizations say few or none of their staff actually understand AI; and a majority of grantmakers operate with no AI policy at all (CEP 2025; TAG 2024). Those are not abstract sector weaknesses. Each one is a specific gap your organization can close — and the order in which you close them matters.

A Note on Method and Sourcing

This is a desk-research synthesis, not original survey work. Every quantitative claim traces to a named, reputable source — sector research bodies (TAG, CEP, Candid), established benchmark reports (TechSoup/Tapp Network), trade press (Devex, Philanthropy News Digest), or primary announcements from the funders and vendors themselves. Where a figure is self-reported by an interested party — a vendor describing its own product, a funder announcing its own giving — it is labeled as such, because self-reported numbers are marketing until independently verified.

Two cautions specific to this topic. First, survey populations differ. TAG surveys grantmaking organizations globally; CEP surveys U.S. foundations and nonprofits; TechSoup/Tapp surveys predominantly U.S. nonprofits; some UK figures enter through the TAG/CAST design-studio work. These are not the same denominator, and a careful reader should not stack their percentages as if they were. Second, “AI use” is a slippery construct. A staffer pasting a paragraph into a chatbot once a month and an organization running a fine-tuned model against its grants database both count as “using AI” in most of these instruments. The high adoption numbers describe breadth of contact, not depth of integration — a distinction this report returns to repeatedly.

Part I — The Adoption Paradox: Everywhere and Nowhere

The single most important fact about AI in philanthropy is that adoption and maturity have decoupled. Contact with AI is nearly universal; disciplined deployment is rare.

On the funder side, TAG’s 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey — reflecting the operational reality of more than 350 foundations globally — found that 81 percent of foundations report some degree of AI usage, while just 4 percent report AI usage across the entire organization (Source: Technology Association of Grantmakers, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey). Read those two numbers together and the picture sharpens: AI has arrived in nearly every foundation as a scattering of individual habits, and in almost none of them as an institutional capability. The 77-point spread between “some usage” and “enterprise usage” is the maturity gap in a single line.

The Center for Effective Philanthropy’s AI With Purpose (September 2025), which the organization describes as the most representative comparative study of AI use in philanthropy and nonprofits to date, triangulates this. Drawing on surveys of 215 foundation leaders and 451 nonprofit leaders plus interviews, CEP found that almost two-thirds of foundations and nonprofits report their organization uses AI, primarily for internal productivity and communications tasks (Source: Center for Effective Philanthropy, AI With Purpose, September 2025, authors Elisha Smith Arrillaga, Seara Grundhoefer, Christina Im). CEP frames the macro backdrop bluntly: business adoption of AI surged from 55 percent in 2023 to nearly 80 percent in 2025, and the nonprofit sector is tracking a similar climb (CEP 2025).

The “primarily for internal productivity and communications” detail matters more than it looks. It tells us where the adoption actually is: drafting, summarizing, rewriting, formatting — the low-risk, high-frequency text work that a general-purpose chatbot does out of the box. It is not, for most organizations, in grant decisioning, impact measurement, or any workflow where a wrong answer carries real consequence. What this means for you: most “AI adoption” in philanthropy today is unmanaged consumer-tool use, which means the sector — and quite possibly your own organization — has accumulated the risk surface of AI (data leakage, inaccuracy, bias) while capturing only the shallowest layer of its value.

On the nonprofit (grantee) side, the TechSoup and Tapp Network State of AI in Nonprofits 2025 benchmark — drawing on more than 1,300 nonprofit professionals — found that 85.6 percent of nonprofits are exploring AI tools, but only 24 percent have a formal AI strategy (Source: TechSoup & Tapp Network, The State of AI in Nonprofits 2025). The same report surfaces a digital divide that should worry anyone who cares about equity in the sector: larger nonprofits, with annual budgets above $1 million, are adopting AI at nearly twice the rate of smaller organizations — 66 percent versus 34 percent (TechSoup/Tapp 2025). AI is not arriving evenly. It is arriving fastest where there is already capacity to absorb it.

Put the three surveys side by side and the paradox is stable across every population measured: near-ubiquitous experimentation, near-absent governance. That is the through-line of this entire report.

Part II — The Dissolving Boundary Between Funder and Field

To understand where the service opportunities are, you have to stop thinking of “philanthropy” as one block. There are two populations on either side of every grant: the foundation (the funder) and the nonprofit (the grantee, the field). Historically these two have had different technology realities, different budgets, and almost no shared infrastructure. AI is now blurring that boundary in three specific ways, and each blur is a place where your organization faces new risk — and new opportunity.

First, AI is collapsing the capability gap — and the funder is now the laggard. Foundations have traditionally been the better-resourced party. But on AI specifically, grantees are often moving faster out of necessity. The nonprofit drowning in grant applications has an acute, daily reason to try an AI drafting tool; the program officer reviewing those applications has a slower, more cautious institutional culture. CEP’s data captures the consequence: only 17 percent of nonprofits report that their foundation funders have engaged them in any conversation about using AI (CEP 2025). The funders are, by their own admission, not at the table. A majority of foundation leaders say their staff has limited-to-no understanding of grantees’ AI attitudes, technical abilities, or ethical concerns (CEP 2025). The party that sets the terms of the relationship is the party that understands the technology least. That inversion is itself a market: someone has to translate between a foundation’s caution and a grantee’s urgency, and neither side currently has the staff to do it.

Second, the grant application itself — the core artifact of the funder-field relationship — is being rebuilt around AI, from both ends. Grantees are using AI to write applications: TechSoup/Tapp found 60 percent of nonprofits express strong interest in AI for grant writing and fundraising, with roughly a quarter already using it for grant writing (TechSoup/Tapp 2025). Simultaneously, funders are deploying AI to read and screen those same applications. When both sides automate opposite ends of the same document, the document stops being a reliable signal of anything. This is precisely the provocation TAG, the Centre for the Acceleration of Social Technology (CAST), and the Institute for Voluntary Action Research put to 27 sector leaders at a 2025 London design studio: the form-based application process is broken (Source: Technology Association of Grantmakers, Grantmaking in the Age of AI — A Design Studio Report, September 2025). The question of what replaces it is wide open — and unanswered questions are where advisory revenue lives.

Third, the wall between “the foundation’s data” and “the grantee’s data” is becoming a governance problem neither side owns. A grantee report contains beneficiary information. A grant application contains organizational and sometimes personal data. The moment either party feeds that material into an AI tool — a consumer chatbot, a grants platform’s new AI feature — a data-handling question is created that crosses the funder-field boundary. Yet TAG found only 45 percent of grantmakers have a data privacy policy and only 46 percent have a data retention and destruction policy (TAG 2024). The infrastructure to answer “where did the grantee’s data go when your program officer summarized it with AI?” largely does not exist. That is a compliance gap sitting directly on the seam between the two populations.

The strategic reframe is this: the most valuable — and most neglected — work is not inside a foundation or inside a nonprofit. It is on the boundary between them — in the application process, the data handoff, and the funder’s near-total absence of a grantee-facing AI posture. The organizations that get ahead will address that seam, not just their own four walls.

Part III — Who Is Using What, and For What Purpose

Adoption statistics describe the weather. This section describes the actual tools in the actual hands. It moves across four layers: what foundations use internally, the grants-management platforms that anchor sector operations, what grantees use, and the marquee funders deploying AI as a grantmaking strategy rather than an office tool.

3.1 — Inside the foundation: the unmanaged consumer layer

The dominant reality of AI inside foundations is the general-purpose chatbot used without a license, a policy, or a record. CEP’s finding that foundations use AI “primarily for internal productivity and communications-related tasks” describes work that ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, and Google Gemini do out of the box: drafting board memos, summarizing long grantee reports, rewriting program descriptions, cleaning up meeting notes, and turning bullet points into prose (CEP 2025). Because TAG found only 4 percent of foundations use AI organization-wide, the inference is that most of this is happening at the level of the individual employee rather than the sanctioned enterprise tool — the so-called “shadow AI” pattern, where staff bring their own tools faster than the institution can govern them (TAG 2024 for the 4% figure; shadow-AI characterization is inferred, not directly stated by TAG).

This matters because shadow AI is the single most common starting condition — and quite possibly your own. You are not starting from a blank slate: you likely have people already using AI in uncontrolled ways, and a leadership team that suspects this is happening but cannot see it. The first move is almost always visibility and rules, not new tools.

3.2 — The grants-management platform layer: AI bolted onto the system of record

The operational spine of most foundations is a grants-management system (GMS), and the major vendors have all moved to add AI features. The established players, by repeated sector comparison, are:

  • Fluxx — positioned for enterprise foundations running complex, multi-stage grant lifecycles, with deeper grant-lifecycle management and analytics, at the cost of significant implementation investment (Source: Plinth, Grant Management Systems Compared, 2025–2026; note Plinth is a competing vendor and its comparative characterizations are interested). Fluxx markets an AI capability (branded “Fluxx AI”) oriented toward summarizing and retrieving information across large volumes of narrative text (Source: Fluxx.io product pages, self-reported). One competitor’s analysis characterizes Fluxx’s AI as fundamentally “a reading and retrieval tool” that helps staff digest information faster rather than evaluate it more rigorously (Source: Plinth comparison — this is a competitor’s characterization and should be treated as interested commentary, not neutral verification).
  • Submittable — faster to deploy, strong out-of-the-box applicant experience, built for high-volume application processing (Plinth 2025–2026).
  • Foundant (GLM) — the most widely recommended mid-market option globally; notably, Foundant now owns SmartSimple, giving it two product tiers across the market (Plinth 2025–2026; Foundant blog).
  • SmartSimple — a highly customizable enterprise-grade platform supporting the full grant lifecycle (Plinth 2025–2026).

The pattern across all of them is consistent and important: most GMS AI features today are text summarization, form auto-fill, and document extraction (Plinth 2025–2026). In plain terms, the platforms are automating reading and data entry, not judgment. The same comparison work flags a structural blind spot — unbundled platforms tend to stop at the award decision, so the rich context captured during an application “effectively disappears” once a grant is funded, leaving post-award monitoring and impact tracking comparatively under-served by AI (Plinth 2025–2026). The implication: the AI layer in foundation software is concentrated at intake and review, and thin at monitoring, learning, and impact — exactly the stages where foundations most want to demonstrate value.

Read the GMS layer carefully if your organization runs one. The platform you already pay for is almost certainly under-used: its AI features are bolted on at intake and review, and the real value is in integration, configuration, and the workflow design that the vendor sells a license for but rarely delivers hands-on. The first question is not “what new tool should we buy” but “what is the system we already own actually capable of, and who will configure it.”

3.3 — The grantee layer: grant-writing and fundraising tools

On the nonprofit side, the use case is sharper and more financially motivated: get more money, faster, with fewer staff. TechSoup/Tapp quantifies the appetite — 60 percent of nonprofits show strong interest in AI for grant writing and fundraising, 24.6 percent are already using AI for grant writing, and 33 percent for content marketing — while only 12.8 percent use predictive analytics, signaling that grantees are adopting the easy generative use cases and leaving the harder, data-intensive ones untouched (TechSoup/Tapp 2025). The tool layer here is a mix of the same general-purpose chatbots plus a growing crop of nonprofit-specific grant-writing assistants. Specific product names in this category proliferate and churn quickly; I am not naming individual grantee-side grant-writing startups here without a stable, reputable source, because the category turns over fast and a name that is accurate today may mislead in 60 days (verify any specific vendor before citing in external output).

3.4 — The strategic funders: AI as the grant, not the tool

A distinct and highly visible layer is the set of foundations for which AI is not an office productivity question but the actual object of their giving. These are worth naming because they shape sector narrative and signal where institutional money is flowing.

  • The Patrick J. McGovern Foundation is the clearest example of AI-native grantmaking at scale. In December 2025 it announced a $75.8 million commitment framed around building “the architecture that guides” AI’s use, prioritizing institutions over breakthroughs (Source: Patrick J. McGovern Foundation press release, December 2025; Devex, McGovern Foundation’s AI grants prioritize institutions over breakthroughs). Its 2025 funding spanned 149 grants across 13 countries (McGovern Foundation, 2025). Named recipients illustrate the applied bent: Digital Green (AI-enabled agricultural advisory for smallholder farmers) and Direct Relief (machine learning for humanitarian medical forecasting) (McGovern Foundation, 2025). Prior-year commitments — $73.5 million in 2024 and an earlier $66.4 million round — establish this as a sustained strategy, not a one-off (McGovern Foundation press releases, 2024).
  • Google.org’s Accelerator: Generative AI is the corporate-philanthropy analogue. Its 2024 inaugural cohort backed 21 organizations expected to reach more than 30 million beneficiaries; the 2025 second cohort selected 20 organizations sharing $30 million in funding — no less than $500,000 each, potentially exceeding $2 million per organization — alongside a six-month accelerator with technical training, Google Cloud credits, and pro-bono Google staff support (Source: Google.org / blog.google, Generative AI Accelerator 2025). Note this is a company deploying its own philanthropic capital to seed adoption of AI built on its own cloud — a legitimate program, and also a distribution strategy (dual-purpose framing is inferred).
  • The KPMG U.S. Foundation committed $6 million in grants to help nonprofits integrate AI into operations (Source: KPMG U.S. Foundation, AI Impact Grant, 2025).

The strategic-funder layer tells the rest of the sector two things. First, there is real institutional conviction and real money behind “AI for social good,” which raises the whole sector’s expectation that organizations should have an AI answer. Second — and this is the gap these large programs do not close — pouring capital into flagship AI projects does almost nothing for the operational AI readiness of the average small foundation or mid-sized nonprofit. The marquee money goes to the frontier; the median organization is still trying to figure out whether staff are allowed to paste a grantee’s report into ChatGPT. That distance between the sector’s AI headlines and its operational floor is exactly where most organizations actually live — and where the unglamorous, practical work of getting AI-ready gets done.

Part IV — Why Most Organizations Will Need Outside Help

There is a wall between organizations that need AI capability and the specialists who can provide it. That wall is already structurally weak, and AI is removing what is left of it. If you are weighing whether to build AI capability in-house or bring it in, three forces are pushing most organizations toward the latter.

Force one: the in-house expertise simply isn’t there, and the sector knows it. The TechSoup/Tapp benchmark states the conclusion in its own section heading — “Limited In-House AI Expertise Highlights Outsourcing Opportunity” (TechSoup/Tapp 2025). This is the sector’s own research telling the sector that it will have to look outside for AI capability. CEP’s parallel finding is that roughly two-thirds of both foundations and nonprofits report that none or only a few of their staff have a solid understanding of AI and its applications (CEP 2025). When two-thirds of an organization cannot evaluate AI, the build-versus-buy question answers itself: there is no one inside to build with. The boundary between “foundation staff” and “outside agency” becomes porous because the foundation cannot staff the work internally even if it wanted to.

Force two: foundation teams are structurally lean. The philanthropic sector runs on small teams carrying large responsibility — most foundations operate with a handful to a few dozen staff managing significant financial and programmatic obligations (general sector characterization consistent with Philanthropy_AI persona domain notes; specific headcount distributions vary). A lean team has no spare capacity to stand up an AI governance program, write a policy, run training, and maintain a compliance posture on top of its existing grant cycle. The work is real, recurring, and not a fit for a new full-time hire at most organizations — which is the exact profile of work that gets outsourced to a specialist.

Force three: AI implementation is not a project, it is a posture that needs maintenance. Models change. Vendors ship new features. Regulations move. A policy written for one model generation can be wrong by the next. This converts what might have been a one-time consulting engagement into an ongoing relationship — the “setup plus maintenance” structure that defines a managed-services model rather than a project-consulting model. The broader AI consulting market reflects this shift: managed services and training-and-support are described as the fastest-growing segments precisely because organizations prefer to outsource ongoing AI oversight to specialized firms rather than carry it internally (Source: Verified Market Reports, AI Consulting Services Market — note this is a market-research vendor and its figures are commercial estimates, not audited data; treat sizing claims as directional).

The net effect is that outside help stops looking like a one-off vendor delivering a discrete artifact and starts looking like an embedded, fractional AI function — your organization’s AI capability, rented rather than hired. For most lean organizations that is the more realistic option: the less AI expertise you can afford to hire and retain in-house, the more it makes sense to rent it, and the longer you are likely to keep renting it. The practical implication is to budget for an ongoing relationship, not a single project.

One discipline the sector’s own culture demands here: treat AI as capacity augmentation, never staff replacement. Foundations are mission-driven, risk-averse, and culturally sensitive to anything that sounds extractive or like automation-for-its-own-sake. The value worth pursuing is time back for mission — the program officer freed from administrative drafting to spend more time with grantees — not headcount reduction. This is not merely good manners; inside a mission-driven organization it is the only framing that will hold internally.

Part V — The Gaps That Matter Most

This is the core of the report. Each gap below is grounded in a specific, sourced statistic, paired with what closing it actually requires. Policy and training are treated first and most fully, because they are the largest and the most consequential to get right.

Gap 1 — The Policy and Governance Vacuum

This is the largest single gap in the sector, and it is quantified from multiple independent directions.

On the funder side, TAG found that only 30 percent of foundations have an AI policy in place, and just 9 percent have both an AI policy and an AI advisory committee (TAG 2024). Restated: roughly 70 percent of foundations are using AI with no policy governing it, and more than 90 percent lack the governance structure (a policy plus a body to steward it) to manage it responsibly. On the grantee side, the numbers are worse — TechSoup/Tapp found 76 percent of nonprofits have no AI policy at all, and Candid’s AI Equity Project work indicates only about 15 percent of nonprofits have an organizational policy for responsible AI use (TechSoup/Tapp 2025; Candid AI Equity Project). The TAG/CAST design studio reinforced the funder picture from the UK angle: even as the overwhelming majority of grantmaking staff experiment with AI, two-thirds of funders still lack policies or governance frameworks (TAG Grantmaking in the Age of AI, 2025).

The reason this gap is so tractable is that it is acute and well-bounded. The organization knows it has no policy. Leadership is anxious about it. And an AI governance framework — acceptable-use rules, data-handling rules, a tool-approval process, human-in-the-loop requirements, an oversight body — is a concrete, finishable deliverable, not an open-ended research problem. What you need here is not more advice; it is an operationalized governance framework your organization can actually run on.

A note on quality, because this gap attracts low-effort shortcuts: free policy templates exist (NTEN publishes a widely used Generative AI Use Policy Template, for instance) (Source: NTEN AI resources). The template is not the solution. A template a lean foundation cannot interpret, adapt to its grants workflow, or enforce is shelfware. The real work is the translation from template to operating reality — and that is precisely what an organization with “few or no staff who understand AI” cannot do for itself.

Gap 2 — The Training and AI-Literacy Gap

The second-largest opportunity, and the one most resistant to commoditization, is human capability. The data is stark and consistent. CEP: roughly two-thirds of foundations and nonprofits say none or only a few of their staff have a solid understanding of AI (CEP 2025). TAG: a lack of necessary skills is the second-ranked barrier to meaningful AI adoption, cited by 43 percent of foundations, behind only privacy and security concerns at 55 percent and ahead of uncertainty about relevant use cases at 40 percent (TAG 2024). Note that two of the top three barriers — skills (43%) and use-case uncertainty (40%) — are literacy problems, not technology problems. They are solved by teaching, not by buying software.

The training gap is more defensible than the policy gap for a simple reason: a policy is a document that can, in principle, be copied; skill transfer cannot. Training that is workflow-specific — teaching a grants manager to use AI safely on actual grant applications, teaching a program officer to summarize grantee reports without leaking beneficiary data — cannot be downloaded. It has to be delivered, in context, by someone who understands both the tool and the foundation’s actual operations. The training worth paying for is practitioner-level and workflow-specific — hands-on workshops grounded in your real tasks — not generic courses, certifications, or a “course catalog” that never touches how your team actually works.

There is a demand-side wrinkle worth flagging honestly. CEP found that over a third of foundations say they have not received many, if any, requests from grantees for AI support (CEP 2025), and staff interest in nonprofits is high while time, training, and budget have not kept pace (Candid AI Equity Project). The inference: demand for training is latent, not always expressed. Organizations need it and want it, but a lean team rarely surfaces “we need AI training” as a budget line on its own. The practical implication: the case for training usually has to be made deliberately — someone has to make the cost of un-governed, untrained AI use legible to leadership before the budget appears. If you recognize the need, expect to advocate for it rather than wait for it to surface on its own.

Gap 3 — The Compliance and Data-Governance Gap

This gap sits underneath the first two and is the one most likely to produce a genuine incident. TAG found only 45 percent of grantmakers have a data privacy policy and only 46 percent have a data retention and destruction policy (TAG 2024). Layer AI on top of that foundation — a workforce pasting grantee and beneficiary data into consumer tools with unknown retention behavior — and the exposure is obvious. Privacy and security concerns are, by the sector’s own ranking, the number-one barrier to AI adoption at 55 percent (TAG 2024), which means the buyer already feels this risk acutely; it does not need to be convinced the problem exists.

Closing this gap calls for a compliance posture assessment and awareness framework — not legal advice or a compliance guarantee. The line between “here is your data-handling risk surface and a framework to manage it” and “you are compliant” is the line between useful help and false comfort. Be wary of anyone offering compliance guarantees or implied legal advice; in a regulated context, those claims require actual counsel. The honest version of this work is awareness, structure, and a clear flag that says “this part needs a lawyer.”

Gap 4 — The Funder-to-Grantee Support Gap (the meta-opportunity)

This is the gap that sits squarely on the boundary between funders and the field, and it is enormous. CEP found that nearly 90 percent of foundations provide no funding or non-monetary support for their grantees’ use of AI, and only 14 percent have concrete plans to increase AI implementation support over the next three years (CEP 2025). Meanwhile nonprofits, asked what AI support they want from funders, name capacity support clearly (CEP 2025). There is, in other words, a documented supply-demand mismatch: grantees want AI capacity support, and funders overwhelmingly are not providing it.

The most promising structure here is a funder-paid one, because of who carries the cost. A foundation that wants to support its grantees’ AI readiness — but has no internal capability to deliver that support — can fund a specialist to deliver it to a cohort of grantees. The foundation gets to act on its mission and its stated equity concerns; the grantees get real capacity; and the cost is shared across many organizations rather than carried by each alone. This is the “capacity-building grant” model applied to AI, and it directly addresses the digital divide (66% vs 34% adoption by budget size) that would otherwise widen (TechSoup/Tapp 2025). This is an inferred model, not a documented market — there is no well-sourced example of it running at scale yet — but every input it requires is independently verified, and it turns the sector’s single largest documented gap into something a funder can actually act on.

Gap 5 — The Post-Award and Impact-Measurement Gap

As noted in Part III, the GMS AI layer is concentrated at intake and review and thin at monitoring, learning, and impact, because unbundled platforms tend to lose application context once a grant is funded (Plinth 2025–2026). Foundations are under perennial pressure to demonstrate impact to boards and donors, and the survey data shows predictive analytics adoption among nonprofits at just 12.8 percent (TechSoup/Tapp 2025) — the hard, data-intensive use cases are the least adopted. The inference is that there is unmet demand for AI applied to the back half of the grant lifecycle — synthesizing grantee reports, surfacing patterns across a portfolio, drafting impact narratives from monitoring data. This is more technical, more bespoke work than policy or training, and fewer providers do it well — but it is where AI can most directly help you demonstrate impact to a board or donor.

Gap 6 — The Equitable-AI Gap

The sector’s stated values create a distinctive service opening. CEP found that half of foundations that provide any AI support do not provide support specifically to advance equitable AI, and over 80 percent of nonprofits report participating in no activities to advance equitable AI (CEP 2025). Candid’s work shows familiarity with AI bias as a concept (around 64 percent) far outrunning the implementation of equity practices (around 36 percent) (Candid AI Equity Project). For a mission-driven sector, the gap between professed values and actual practice on equitable AI is both a credibility risk and an opening to act — bias auditing, inclusive-design review, and equity-aware governance all align with what foundations already say they care about. The honest caveat: this is values-aligned work that organizations say they want more than they currently invest in, so it is best folded into a governance or training effort rather than pursued as a standalone initiative.

Part VI — Where to Get Help (and How to Choose)

There are two broad kinds of provider you can turn to. They draw on the same gaps but operate very differently, with different strengths, costs, and risks. Conflating them leads to a poor hiring decision, so it is worth understanding each before you choose.

Option A — The Specialist AI-Implementation Firm

This is the firm whose entire focus is helping an organization adopt AI responsibly: governance, training, compliance posture, and ongoing optimization. What to expect:

Where you’d start. If this is you — an executive director, COO, or operations lead at a small-to-mid foundation or nonprofit who feels the anxiety of un-governed AI use and has no one internal to address it — the natural entry point is the cheapest, most acute pain, usually the policy vacuum (Gap 1), which then opens the door to training (Gap 2) and compliance (Gap 3). A good specialist offers several entry points into a single ongoing relationship rather than forcing an all-or-nothing engagement.

What it costs you. Expect a setup-plus-maintenance shape. The initial engagement (policy framework, baseline training, compliance assessment) is a discrete, scoped deliverable; the ongoing relationship (policy updates as models change, refresher training, posture re-assessment) is a recurring cost. This is the managed-services structure the broader AI consulting market is converging on (Verified Market Reports, directional), and it reflects a real fact about AI: it needs maintenance, not a one-time install.

What makes a good one worth keeping. Three things. First, workflow specificity — training and configuration delivered in the context of your actual operations cannot be templated away. Second, sector fluency — understanding foundation culture, grant lifecycles, IRS 990-PF realities, and grantee-data sensitivity is a real barrier for a generalist AI consultant. Third, the maintenance relationship — once embedded as your fractional AI function, the right firm becomes the natural partner for every subsequent need, which is convenient but also worth periodically pressure-testing.

What to watch for. Your own demand may be latent — you need this more than you currently feel you do — so a good firm will spend time making the cost of un-governed AI use legible to your leadership, not just selling a deliverable. Expect the process to be deliberate; a risk-averse, mission-driven organization should move carefully here. And insist on current expertise: a firm that has fallen behind the model and regulatory frontier is selling stale governance, which is worse than none.

Red flags. Walk away from any firm that promises ROI guarantees without substantiated data, compliance guarantees, or anything that amounts to legal advice. All three are overreach — and in a sector that will not forgive it, all three are liability traps for you as much as for the provider.

Option B — Providers Who Add AI to an Existing Relationship

This is the wider field of organizations that serve philanthropy and can add AI to a service relationship you already have, rather than treating AI implementation as their whole offer. It is a larger, more crowded, and more fragmented field. It includes:

Grants consultants and philanthropic advisory firms. Already trusted advisors on strategy, grant design, and operations, these firms can attach AI governance and training to an existing retainer. Their advantage is the incumbent relationship; their disadvantage is that AI is a sideline for them, so depth is often shallow. They are more likely to partner with a specialist (Option A) than to build the capability themselves — so you may end up working with both.

Grants-management platform vendors and their implementation partners. Fluxx, Submittable, Foundant, and SmartSimple are all adding AI features (Part III), and their implementation/configuration partners are positioned to sell the services layer around those features. Their advantage is proximity to the system of record; their constraint is that their AI story is bounded by their product’s roadmap. They sell their AI, not AI strategy. What they structurally cannot give you is cross-platform, vendor-neutral advice — for that you need someone who is not tied to a single product.

Philanthropy-serving organizations (PSOs) and membership bodies. TAG, Council on Foundations, PEAK Grantmaking, Candid, and NTEN are producing frameworks, templates, and convenings (the TAG design studio, NTEN’s policy template, Candid’s AI Equity Project, Project Evident’s AI Journey Map for philanthropy) (multiple sources cited above). These bodies create the standards and awareness layer. They are an excellent free starting point — frameworks, templates, and convenings you can use directly — but they generally provide the map, not the hands-on implementation. Use them to orient yourself and to sanity-check any provider’s approach, then bring in help to actually execute.

Managed IT and digital-transformation providers moving up-market. Generalist nonprofit-tech and managed-service providers are adding AI to their menus. Their advantage is existing infrastructure relationships and scale; their disadvantage is the absence of foundation-specific fluency and a tendency to sell tools over governance.

The practical difference is this: Option A gives you depth and sector fluency in a focused niche; Option B gives you breadth, an existing relationship, and bundling. For most organizations the right answer is a combination — a specialist for the core governance, training, and compliance work, and your existing providers for the pieces that ride alongside what they already do. What to watch over the next 12–24 months: whether a large platform vendor or national consultancy builds a serious philanthropy-AI practice that offers both depth and breadth, which would give you a more consolidated option than exists today.

Part VII — What Could Shift, and What This Report Does Not Know

Intellectual honesty requires marking the edges of the evidence.

Time-sensitive items (— likely to move within 30–60 days):

  • Adoption percentages. Every survey cited here is a snapshot from 2024–2025. With business AI adoption having moved from 55 to ~80 percent in two years (CEP 2025), the philanthropy figures are near-certain to be higher by the time this is read. The shape of the finding (broad use, thin governance) is more durable than any specific number.
  • Vendor AI features. GMS platforms are shipping AI capabilities continuously. Any specific feature characterization (including the Fluxx “retrieval not evaluation” framing, which is itself a competitor’s claim) may be outdated quickly. Verify current product capabilities directly before relying on them with a client.
  • Funder programs. The McGovern, Google.org, and KPMG figures are point-in-time announcements; new cohorts and commitments will supersede them.

What this report does not know (explicit gaps):

  • I do not have a reliable, sourced figure for the size of the philanthropy-specific AI-services market. The AI-consulting growth figures cited come from a commercial market-research vendor and are directional at best; they are not philanthropy-specific. Treat any market-sizing as unverified.
  • I have not named specific grantee-side grant-writing AI vendors because the category churns and I lack a stable reputable source; naming them would risk being wrong, which the accuracy standard treats as worse than silence.
  • The funder-paid grantee-capacity model (Gap 4) is an inferred business model, not a documented one. Its inputs are verified; its existence as a scaled, named practice is not. It is a hypothesis worth testing, not a proven market.
  • Causation behind the digital divide (larger orgs adopting at 2x the rate) is not established by the data — budget is correlated with adoption, but the mechanism (staff capacity? risk tolerance? infrastructure?) is inferred.
  • Most figures are U.S.-weighted. TAG is global and some design-studio data is UK-sourced, but the bulk of the quantitative picture reflects U.S. organizations and may not generalize internationally.

These caveats are not hedging for its own sake. A foundation board is a skeptical audience, and anyone — you, or a provider pitching you — who walks in with overclaimed numbers loses the room. The defensible posture is to lead with the verified shape of the problem and flag the soft numbers as soft.

What This Means for You

The philanthropy sector has bought the tools and skipped the discipline. That sentence is the entire thesis. Four in five foundations use AI; one in twenty-five uses it organization-wide. A majority have no policy. Two-thirds of staff cannot evaluate the technology they are already using. Nearly nine in ten foundations offer their grantees no AI support at all. Each of those is a sourced fact, and each is a gap you can act on.

What it means for you depends on where you sit. If you run or operate a foundation or nonprofit, the three documented gaps the sector cannot self-serve — policy, training, and compliance — are the three things to put in place first, and roughly in that order: a governance policy you can actually run on, workflow-specific training so staff use AI without leaking sensitive data, and a compliance posture that names your data-handling risks honestly. The structural forces (no in-house expertise, lean teams, maintenance rather than one-time projects) mean most organizations will rent this capability rather than build it — so plan for an ongoing relationship, not a one-off project, and hold to the cultural line that AI augments capacity rather than replacing staff. And if you are a funder, the single largest gap — the funder-to-grantee support void, where 90 percent of foundations do nothing and 14 percent plan to — is the highest-leverage place to act: funding cohort AI support for your grantees turns the sector’s biggest weakness into measurable mission impact.

The discipline, drawn from the sector’s own evidence: start from the verified shape of the problem rather than soft numbers or vendor hype, choose help that matches the gap (a specialist for depth, your existing providers for the adjacent pieces), and be wary of anyone who overclaims on ROI, compliance, or legal ground. This is a sector built to distrust hype, and rightly so. The opportunity to use AI well here is real and well-documented. The way to squander it is to keep adding tools without the discipline to govern them.
