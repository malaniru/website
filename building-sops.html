Building SOPs: Making the Gains Durable

The last step of the adoption path turns the team's hard-won AI know-how into documented, repeatable procedures — so the capability belongs to the organization, not to whoever happens to be in the room.

It is also the most natural step, because by this point the team has already built the raw material: prompts that work. A standard operating procedure is what those prompts become once they're written down, checked, and made repeatable.

An SOP turns one person's knowledge into something the whole team can follow.

A standard operating procedure is a written record of how a particular piece of work actually gets done — the steps, the order they happen in, the inputs they depend on, and the checks that confirm the result is right before it leaves the building.

Most small organizations run on undocumented procedure. The work gets done because a few experienced people know how, hold the context, and quietly fill the gaps. That works until someone is on vacation, a role turns over, the volume doubles, or the founder isn't in the room. The capability was never the organization's — it belonged to a person, and it left when their attention did.

Why It Matters

Repeatable, consistent, and improvable

An SOP closes that gap. It makes a procedure repeatable by anyone with the right access, consistent from one run to the next, and improvable over time — because a written process can be reviewed and corrected in a way that a habit cannot.

In an AI-enabled workflow it carries one more thing: it records where a human has to look before the work is considered done. That review checkpoint is part of the procedure, not an afterthought to it.

From Prompts to SOPs

How a set of prompts becomes an SOP

When a team starts using AI in earnest, the first durable thing they build is rarely a policy or a tool. It's a prompt that works — a carefully worded instruction that reliably turns a known input into the output someone needs: the grant summary in the right format, the intake email triaged the way the team would triage it, the report section drafted the way the organization actually writes. A good prompt is a procedure in disguise. Turning it into an SOP happens in four deliberate stages.

1

Capture what works

Which prompts reliably produce the right result?

What it does

A prompt that produced the right result gets written down, named, and stored where the team can find it — rather than reconstructed from memory each time. This is the moment a lucky one-off becomes a reusable asset.

2

Write the procedure around it

What does this prompt need to run, and in what order?

What it does

The procedure is written around the prompt: what input it expects and in what form, which prompt to run and in what order when a task takes more than one, and what a correct result looks like. The prompt is the engine; the SOP is the operating manual that says how and when to use it.

3

Make the human checkpoint explicit

Who reviews the output, and what are they checking for?

What it does

The SOP states plainly who reviews the AI's output, what they are checking for, and which decisions are never handed to the model at all. This is the line between a procedure that scales safely and one that quietly compounds errors — a wrong answer that goes out unreviewed is worse than no answer.

4

Keep it alive

Does this procedure still describe reality?

What it does

Models change, the work changes, and a prompt that was reliable six months ago may need adjusting. An SOP reviewed on a cadence stays trustworthy; one written once and forgotten slowly stops describing the work it's supposed to govern.

Why This Is The Last Step

Capability that stays with the organization

The result is that the team's hard-won knowledge — the wording, the judgment, the sequence that took real effort to get right — stops being personal and becomes organizational. That is what makes the gains from AI adoption durable rather than temporary.

When people are busy

The procedure runs the same way under pressure as it does on a quiet day, because it doesn't depend on anyone holding the whole process in their head.

When staff change

The capability stays when someone leaves. A new team member follows the documented procedure instead of relearning it from scratch.

When the founder isn't in the room

The work no longer waits on one person's attention. The organization owns the know-how, with the review checkpoints built in.
