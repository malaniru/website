Executive Summary

Two of the largest AI-for-good funding vehicles in U.S. philanthropy are open or imminent as this issue publishes: the OpenAI Foundation's People-First AI Fund is accepting applications through July 15, 2026, and Humanity AI's $10 million open call is due to launch this summer (✅ Verified). Both point the money at community-rooted nonprofits rather than AI labs. Underneath the headline grants, the sector's core problem has not moved: the best current benchmark finds roughly nine in ten nonprofits touching AI in some form but only a small single-digit share reporting real strategic impact, with training budgets and written policies still rare (⚠️ vendor-sponsored benchmark). Grantmaking platforms keep adding AI features faster than foundations are governing their own use of grantee data. This is the first issue in a weekly series, so it establishes a baseline; where an item predates the past seven days but remains live and actionable, it is dated explicitly.

1. Funding & Grants

The most actionable item for any nonprofit reading this is a live deadline. On June 15, 2026, the OpenAI Foundation announced a $50 million commitment to its 2026 People-First AI Fund, with applications open through July 15, 2026 at 11:59 PM PT (✅ Verified — OpenAI Foundation). The grants are unrestricted, capped at roughly 10% of an organization's annual budget, and open to U.S.-based 501(c)(3)s with operating budgets between $500,000 and $10 million; the Foundation says it expects to prioritize organizations in the $1–8 million range. This year's fund adds eligibility for certain community foundations and regranting organizations, and it is explicitly platform-agnostic — applicants do not need to use OpenAI products, and grants include no free credits. Notifications are expected by October 2026. Three focus areas frame the round: community support services, community arts and cultural organizations, and community journalism and media.

What This Means for You: the eligibility floor and ceiling are strict. If your operating budget sits between roughly $1 million and $8 million and you can evidence deep community trust plus a genuine interest in AI, this is a rare source of unrestricted money — but the window closes in under two weeks from this issue's date.

The second vehicle is pooled and larger in ambition. Humanity AI — the $500 million, five-year initiative launched in October 2025 and co-chaired by the MacArthur Foundation and Omidyar Network, with founding partners including Ford, Mellon, Mozilla, Kapor, Doris Duke, Lumina, Packard, and Siegel Family Endowment — announced on May 12, 2026 more than $18 million in new grantmaking (✅ Verified — Ford Foundation). Of that, roughly $8 million went to inaugural grantees: ten organizations at $500,000 each (among them AI Now Institute, the Center for Democracy and Technology, Partnership on AI, the Pulitzer Center, and TechEquity), plus $3 million to a Data & Society–led "AI Civics" project run through libraries with the Digital Public Library of America. The remaining $10 million is committed to an open call the collaborative says it will launch this summer, with focus areas and timeline still to be published (✅ Verified — Ford Foundation). For grant-seekers, this is the one to watch over the coming weeks.

A smaller but genuinely current item sits at the AI-safety end of the field: Grantmaking.ai, in partnership with the regranting platform Manifund, is distributing about $1 million for AI existential-safety work, with a July 13, 2026 deadline to guarantee review and a final close of July 27, 2026 (🔶 Inferred — platform self-description; figures not independently audited). It is a narrow niche, but the dates are inside the current window.

2. Nonprofit Adoption & Literacy

The adoption-versus-impact gap remains the defining story, and the newest benchmark sharpens it. The 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report, a study of 346 organizations published by the fundraising-software firm Virtuous, reports that 92% of nonprofits use AI in some capacity but only 7% say it has produced major strategic impact (⚠️ vendor-sponsored benchmark — Virtuous, via NonProfit PRO). The same report puts 47% of nonprofits with no AI policy at all and only 4% with an AI-specific training budget; among organizations not yet using AI, 48% cite lack of training and 44% want help simply getting started. Because Virtuous sells tools into this market, treat the precise percentages as directional rather than settled — but the shape matches every prior sector survey: broad contact, shallow integration.

The most concrete literacy response is a training push, not a tool. The AI for Nonprofits Sprint, run by the Fund for the City of New York, aims to bring 100,000 staff across 1,000 nonprofits to basic AI literacy in 2026, up from 38,000 staff across 139 organizations in its first year (which began in Fall 2024); the program is backed by OpenAI, the Robin Hood Foundation, and others (✅ Verified — AI for Nonprofits Sprint / FCNY). Its framing is worth noting: it treats AI adoption as change-management and culture work, targeting a threshold of 50%+ of staff at an organization reaching literacy rather than isolated power users.

What This Means for You: the data and the Sprint's model agree on the same lesson — the binding constraint is people, not software. An organization that funds training and a written policy before buying more tools is statistically more likely to move from "someone uses ChatGPT" to measurable impact. If a training budget line does not exist yet, that is the cheapest high-leverage change available.

3. Sector Tooling & Platforms

Grantmaking software vendors continued to fold AI into the funder workflow through the first half of 2026. Bonterra launched Bonterra Grantmaker on February 19, 2026, an AI-enabled platform pitched at small and mid-sized private foundations to automate intake-to-decision workflows "without adding staff" (🔶 vendor announcement — Bonterra via Business Wire). It follows Bonterra Que, which the company marketed in October 2025 as an agentic AI platform spanning funders, nonprofits, and supporters (⚠️ self-reported "first," unverifiable). Separately, Candid — formed from the GuideStar and Foundation Center merger — is preparing a next-generation platform that combines GuideStar nonprofit profiles with Foundation Directory funder records and adds machine-learning recommendations (🔶 trade-press reporting; timeline not independently confirmed).

The common pattern across these products is automation of the same four steps: matching organizations to opportunities, auto-populating applications from existing data, drafting narrative text, and flagging compliance risks before submission. None of that is neutral. Auto-drafting and auto-scoring move judgment about who gets funded closer to a model, which raises the governance questions in the next section.

What This Means for You: before adopting an AI grant-review or intake tool, ask the vendor two questions in writing — what happens to applicant data fed into the model, and whether any part of the scoring is automated. A tool that cannot answer both plainly is a governance liability, however good the demo looks.

4. Governance & Data Practice for Grantmakers

Funders are more anxious about their own AI use than their tooling spend would suggest. In a November 2025 Bonterra report, 91% of funders said they see AI transforming philanthropy while 92% said they worry about data use and ethics (⚠️ vendor-sponsored survey — Bonterra). That tension — enthusiastic adoption paired with unresolved data concern — is the governance headline, and it is not being closed by policy at the same rate tools are being bought.

A few foundations are modeling more disciplined practice. The David and Lucile Packard Foundation has been piloting an internal AI chatbot that answers staff questions about grantmaking process and policy, keeping the use case inside its own walls rather than pushing sensitive grantee data into external models (🔶 Inferred — Alliance magazine reporting). The broader principle emerging across sector commentary is straightforward: sensitive data entrusted by applicants and grantees should not be fed into general-purpose AI systems without explicit consent and a clear handling policy, because a foundation holds that data in trust for communities that did not agree to train a model.

What This Means for You: the minimum defensible governance posture for a grantmaker adopting AI is three written commitments — what applicant data may enter an AI system, whose consent is required, and whether a human makes every funding decision. None of that requires a large budget. It requires a decision, and most foundations have not yet made it.
