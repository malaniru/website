1. AI Market Trends

The dominant movement this run is the resolution of the week's two biggest threads — the record chip listing and the second production frontier — into confirmed facts rather than pending events.

First, the SK Hynix debut is now history, and it was strong. Shares rose about 13% on Friday to close near $168.01, up from the $149 ADR pricing, on demand the company's chairman described to CNBC as "enormous"; the roughly $26.5 billion raise stands as the largest US first-time share sale by a foreign issuer (Source: CNBC, July 10; Fast Company, July 10 — press-reported). Two leveraged single-stock ETFs tied to the name, SKUU and SKDD, are expected to launch July 13 (Source: GlobeNewswire, July 8 — press release; confirm listing). The debut lands into an unusually cross-current market: the memory/HBM segment the AI trade has been rotating toward is exactly where the early-July semiconductor selloff started.

Second, GPT-5.6 is a day into GA. All three tiers — Sol (flagship), Terra (balanced), Luna (fastest/cheapest) — are generally available across ChatGPT, ChatGPT Work, Codex, and the API, with API pricing at Sol $5/$30, Terra $2.50/$15, and Luna $1/$6 per million tokens (Source: iClarified, "OpenAI Launches GPT-5.6 … for General Availability," July 10; Dataconomy, July 10 — press/vendor; verify pricing against OpenAI's own page before external use). Note the coverage is not fully consistent: OpenAI's own preview page and some outlets still describe an initial ~20-organization limited-preview posture with a broad release "in the coming weeks," while GA-focused coverage reports full availability (Source: OpenAI, "Previewing GPT-5.6 Sol"; VentureBeat, July — vendor/press). The weight of July 9–10 reporting treats GPT-5.6 as generally available; treat the exact rollout breadth as still settling. Sam Altman's headline claim is a 54% token-efficiency gain on coding tasks for Sol — vendor-stated, unaudited (Source: Dataconomy, July 10 — vendor).

Third, the semiconductor complex is still digesting its reset, and the trigger is instructive. Multiple outlets tie the early-July rout to a report that SK Hynix plans to slow high-bandwidth-memory (HBM) capacity expansion and tilt toward lower-cost commodity DRAM — a demand-signal worry, not a technology failure — with Micron leading one leg down (reported about −13%), Intel about −9%, AMD about −7%, and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF off about 5% (Source: Intellectia.ai, "AI Chip Stocks Plunge," July 2026; Forbes, July 8 — press-reported; figures vary by outlet and window, re-confirm against a live feed). Nvidia continues to trade apart from the group, reported up roughly 6% on the week while the semi ETF fell about 8%, and closing about 0.7% higher at $196.93 on July 7 (Source: 24/7 Wall St., July 9; GuruFocus, July — press-reported). The consistent read across coverage: a valuation-and-sentiment reset on AI-capex-return skepticism and a hawkish Fed, not a collapse in demand.

Fourth, the enterprise-enablement and protocol layers kept building. Microsoft's MCP push continued — a catalog of 60+ ready MCP servers spanning Microsoft 365 Copilot, Copilot Studio, Azure AI Foundry, and GitHub Copilot (Source: Microsoft Power Platform blog, July 6 — vendor). ICML 2026 opened July 6 in Seoul with a record 23,918 submissions and an unusually heavy agentic-AI emphasis across its workshop program — a signal of where the research frontier is pointing (Source: conference reporting, July — press). The throughline holds from earlier in the week: capital and attention are concentrating on the operational and governance layer — deployment, connectors, agent reliability — as much as on the models themselves.
