AI Implementation Trends in the Philanthropy Sector

By Marco Harris·March 20, 2026

An analysis of how foundations are actually using AI — the use cases showing traction, the governance gap, the vendor landscape, and the $500 million Humanity AI initiative positioning philanthropy as an AI stakeholder.

← All philanthropy & AI research

Executive Summary

AI adoption in philanthropy is broad but shallow. The TAG 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey found that 81 percent of foundations report some degree of AI usage, yet only 4 percent have adopted AI across their entire organization (Source: TAG, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, October 2024). The sector is in an experimentation phase — individual staff members are trying AI tools for specific tasks, but enterprise-wide strategy, governance, and integration remain rare.

The most promising implementation patterns are emerging in grant application processing, grantee reporting, document synthesis, and board materials preparation. But the most significant development in 2025 was not a foundation’s internal adoption — it was the launch of Humanity AI, a $500 million five-year initiative backed by ten major foundations to ensure AI development centers human needs (Source: MacArthur Foundation, October 2025).

1. The Current State: Experimentation Without Strategy

The TAG 2024 survey provides the clearest picture: 81 percent report some AI usage, but only 4 percent have enterprise-wide adoption. Thirty percent have an AI policy; 9 percent have both a policy and an advisory committee. Community foundations lead on governance at 33 percent (Source: TAG, 2024).

AI adoption among nonprofits broadly increased from 31 percent in 2024 to 48 percent in 2025 (Source: Momentive Software, 2025). The primary barriers remain privacy and security concerns (55 percent), lack of necessary skills (43 percent), and uncertainty about relevant use cases (40 percent) (Source: TAG, 2024).

2. Use Cases Showing Traction

Grant Application Processing. Foundations are using AI for completeness checks, eligibility verification, coding/tagging, and generating preliminary summaries for reviewers (Source: Foundant, 2025; PEAK Grantmaking, 2025). Only 1 percent use AI for actual screening decisions, and 65 percent have no plans to (Source: Candid, 2025).

Grantee Reporting. The Houston Endowment piloted AI-assisted oral reporting — using transcription tools to generate structured reports from conversations rather than requiring written submissions. The pilot found improved data quality and has since expanded to additional program areas (Source: Chronicle of Philanthropy, 2025).

Document Synthesis and Board Preparation. AI-assisted summarization of grantee reports, evaluation data, and board briefing materials is the most common informal use case. AI can compile impact narratives from grantee reports for donor-facing communications (Source: One Hundred Nights, 2025).

Administrative Tasks. Drafting communications, summarizing meeting notes, and preparing agenda materials — low-risk, high-return starting points.

3. The Vendor Landscape

Foundant Technologies offers AI-powered review summaries with explicit data privacy commitments (Source: Foundant, 2025). CommunityForce positions AI features as competitive differentiators including document intelligence and qualitative data analysis (Source: CommunityForce, 2025). Fluxx remains the most widely used GMS platform with active AI feature development (Source: Fluxx, 2024).

Foundations evaluating GMS platforms should ask specific questions about AI features: what data is processed, whether it is used for model training, and where it is stored.

4. Philanthropy as an AI Stakeholder: Humanity AI

In October 2025, ten major foundations launched Humanity AI — a $500 million five-year initiative. Participating foundations include Ford, MacArthur, Mellon, Packard, Mozilla, Doris Duke, Lumina, Kapor, Omidyar Network, and Siegel Family Endowment. Rockefeller Philanthropy Advisors serves as fiscal sponsor (Source: MacArthur Foundation, 2025).

Early grantees include the National Black Tech Ecosystem Association, AI Now, and a Howard Law School initiative on AI and civil rights (Source: Washington Post, October 2025).

This initiative positions philanthropy as an active shaper of AI governance — not just a consumer. For smaller foundations, it creates a resource stream of research, frameworks, and governance models.

5. Recommendations

The 81 percent adoption rate is a starting point. Foundations should establish AI policies defining approved tools, acceptable data categories, and human review requirements. Staff capacity building should focus on evaluation and governance, not just tool usage. Vendor AI features should be evaluated with the same rigor as core platform functionality.
