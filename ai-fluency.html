A wrong answer is three times worse than no answer.

That is the standard the whole program runs on. The most dangerous moment with AI is the one right after it gives you a great result — fluent, confident, and possibly wrong — because that is exactly when people stop checking.

This training builds the opposite habit: use AI for speed and for the shape of the work, and verify every claim before it leaves your desk. What your team gains is a working, skeptical fluency — one that knows exactly where the tool breaks and how to catch it.

The Four Competencies

A framework for working with AI, taught in order

Anthropic's AI Fluency framework defines four competencies for working effectively with AI. We teach them in sequence, because each one depends on the one before it — and we weight the last two most heavily, since a confident-but-wrong output reaching a client is the expensive failure this training exists to prevent.

1

Delegation

Should AI do this at all — and what stays mine?

What it builds

The most important AI decision is often the decision not to use it, or to use it for only part of a task. Participants learn to split any job three ways: the model's part (the form, the first draft, the reshaping), what stays strictly theirs (the judgment, the relationship, the final call), and the return trip — the point where the draft comes back for checking. A delegation with no return trip isn't delegation; it's abdication.

2

Description

How do I tell it clearly what I actually need?

What it builds

A prompt is a delegation brief, not a magic incantation — and this cohort already knows how to brief people. Participants learn to write clear, well-scoped instructions with role, task, input, output spec, and guardrails, then to iterate specifically rather than vaguely. The signature habit: tell the model to flag what it doesn't know rather than fill the gap — turning its worst instinct, confident invention, into a usable signal.

3

Discernment

Is this output actually good — or just confident?

What it builds

Fluency is not accuracy. A correct answer and a confidently wrong one look identical on the page, and the polish is the disguise. Participants learn to read past it — to inspect facts, numbers, names, dates, and citations against a real source; to notice when the model is simply agreeing with them; and to ask what an expert would have flagged that the output didn't. This is the highest-value skill in the program, and it turns a professional's native skepticism into a repeatable method.

4

Diligence

Am I using this responsibly, in policy, and verifiably?

What it builds

Diligence is what makes the other three safe to use on sensitive material. Participants learn to keep every AI use inside their organization's data-handling rules, to be transparent about where AI contributed, and to be able to show their work — accounting for what the AI did, what was checked, and what was rejected. It is the competency that earns the right to use AI on real client work at all.

How It's Delivered

Practice-based, philanthropy-native, hands-on

Fluency develops through repeated practice, not a single intensive. The program is structured around your team's real work and the verification habit that makes AI safe to rely on.

Your work, not generic exercises

Every example is a workflow your team already recognizes — site visit memos, board book prep, due-diligence write-ups, grantee correspondence, evaluation synthesis. Practice runs on your own de-identified material.

Multi-session, spaced over a few weeks

Delivered across several working sessions with practice in between, so the habits set in rather than fading after a one-day workshop.

Tiered to the team

Starts with the fundamentals everyone needs, builds to practitioner-level skill on real deliverables, with optional advanced depth for messy, multi-source data work.

The gate is a habit, not a quiz

Participants advance by producing one verified piece of real work — a clean transformation checked against its source — not by passing a test. The demonstrated habit is the standard.

What Your Team Walks Away With

Faster on the form, sharper on the facts

The program is assessed on one habit: use AI fast and skeptically, and be able to prove what you checked.

The verification log

A simple, repeatable record of what the AI did, what was checked against which source, and what was accepted, corrected, or rejected. The proof and the habit in one artifact.

A method for catching fabrication

The ability to spot a subtly wrong number or an invented citation buried in otherwise-correct text — before it reaches a client.

In-policy use from day one

AI habits that respect data-handling rules by default, so speed never comes at the cost of a client relationship.
