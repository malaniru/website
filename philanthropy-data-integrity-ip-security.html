Data Integrity and IP-Based Security in the Philanthropic Sector

By Marco Harris·March 14, 2026

A comprehensive examination of cybersecurity threats, intellectual property vulnerabilities, and expanding regulatory obligations facing foundations and philanthropic organizations.

← All philanthropy & AI research
This article is research and sector analysis — not legal advice. Organizations in regulated environments should have findings reviewed by qualified legal or compliance counsel before acting on them.

Executive Summary

Philanthropic organizations — private foundations, community foundations, donor-advised fund managers, and grant-making nonprofits — occupy an increasingly uncomfortable position in the cybersecurity landscape. They hold large volumes of sensitive data (grantee personal information, donor financial records, beneficiary demographics, proprietary program methodologies) while operating with chronically under-resourced IT functions. The convergence of escalating cyber threats, expanding state privacy regulation, and growing intellectual property exposure creates a compound risk that most foundations have not adequately addressed.

This research examines three intersecting dimensions of the problem: data integrity threats specific to the philanthropic sector, IP-based security vulnerabilities (encompassing both intellectual property protection and network-layer IP address security), and the regulatory and framework landscape that governs how foundations should respond.

1. The Threat Landscape: Why Philanthropy Is Uniquely Exposed

1.1 Attack Volume and Targeting

Nonprofits are now the second most targeted sector for cyberattacks, behind only energy, according to Okta’s “Nonprofits at Work 2025” report. Weekly cyberattacks on nonprofits increased 30% year-over-year in 2024 (Integrity360, 2024). Despite this exposure, 56% of nonprofits have no dedicated cybersecurity budget (CyberPeace Institute, 2024).

The disparity between threat exposure and defensive investment is particularly acute in the philanthropic subsector, where lean staffing models — often three to thirty employees managing tens or hundreds of millions in assets — leave little room for dedicated security personnel.

1.2 The Data at Stake

Foundations routinely handle data categories that carry both regulatory and ethical obligations.

Grantee data includes personally identifiable information of grant applicants and recipients — legal names, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, contact information, demographic data, and in some cases health or financial records tied to program eligibility. A breach of this data does not merely create a compliance problem; for foundations serving vulnerable populations, it can threaten physical safety and program participation (Protect Democracy, 2024).

Donor data includes credit card numbers, bank routing information, wealth screening data, and giving history. This data is both monetizable by attackers and protected under an expanding patchwork of state privacy laws (Fundraise Up, 2024).

Proprietary program data includes evaluation methodologies, grant-making frameworks, impact measurement tools, and internal research — intellectual property that differentiates a foundation’s approach and represents years of institutional investment.

1.3 The Blackbaud Case: A Sector-Defining Breach

The 2020 Blackbaud data breach remains the most consequential cybersecurity event in philanthropic history. Blackbaud, which provides data management software to more than 25,000 nonprofit customers worldwide, experienced a phishing-initiated breach that exposed unencrypted data — including bank account information, Social Security numbers, and passwords — from an estimated 1.5 billion donor and patient records across more than 13,000 organizations (FTC, May 2024).

The financial consequences were substantial: a $49.5 million multistate settlement with 49 attorneys general and DC (New York AG, October 2023), a $6.75 million settlement with California for alleged HIPAA and state privacy violations (California AG, June 2024), a $3 million SEC settlement for misleading disclosures about the breach’s scope (SEC, 2023), and an FTC order requiring Blackbaud to delete data it no longer needs to provide services (FTC, May 2024).

For foundations, the Blackbaud breach illustrated a critical structural vulnerability: the sector’s heavy reliance on a small number of specialized vendors concentrates third-party risk. When a single vendor serves thousands of foundations, a single breach cascades across the entire sector.

1.4 Emerging Threat Vectors

Three categories of emerging threats are particularly relevant to philanthropy in 2025–2026.

AI-driven attacks now account for 16% of data breaches, including sophisticated phishing and deepfake impersonation (IBM, Cost of a Data Breach Report, 2025). For foundations, where wire transfers for grants and investments are routine, the risk of AI-generated impersonation targeting financial workflows is significant.

Third-party and supply chain risk has doubled year-over-year, with third-party involvement in breaches reaching 30% (Verizon, 2025 Data Breach Investigations Report). Foundations rely extensively on external platforms — grants management systems, CRMs, financial platforms, and cloud storage — each of which represents a potential vector.

Ransomware targeting smaller organizations has intensified, with 88% of small and midsize organization breaches involving ransomware (Verizon, 2025 DBIR). Foundations in the $5M–$100M asset range, which typically lack dedicated IT security staff, fall squarely in this target zone.

2. IP-Based Security: Two Dimensions

2.1 Intellectual Property Protection

Foundations generate significant intellectual property that often goes unprotected.

Program methodologies and evaluation frameworks represent years of institutional investment. These are trade secrets under intellectual property law, but most foundations have no formal process for identifying, cataloging, or protecting them (Aaron Hall, 2024).

Common failures include lack of copyright notice on publications, lack of ownership notice on domain names and websites, and lack of IP ownership clauses in consultant and author contracts (Hurwit & Associates, 2024).

Research and data assets generated or funded by foundations have both intellectual and commercial value. The Research Foundation for SUNY breach demonstrated that research-adjacent foundations face particular risk: sponsored program documentation, grant applications, and award management systems were all potentially compromised (BotCrawl, 2025).

Best practices for IP protection in the philanthropic sector include conducting regular IP asset inventories, executing non-disclosure agreements with all partners and vendors, incorporating IP ownership clauses in employment and contractor agreements, using secure digital storage and transmission protocols with restricted access, and developing IP insurance policies (Number Analytics, 2025; Barker Brettell, 2024).

2.2 Network-Layer IP Address Security and Access Controls

The current reality for most foundations is that donor databases, grants management systems, and financial platforms are accessible from any internet connection with valid credentials. Remote and hybrid work arrangements have expanded the attack surface further (Reintivity Solutions, 2025).

IP allowlisting — restricting system access to specific IP addresses or ranges — provides a basic but meaningful layer of network security. For foundations, this means configuring grants management systems, CRMs, and financial platforms to accept connections only from known office networks, approved VPN endpoints, or verified remote work locations.

Zero Trust Architecture represents the more comprehensive approach. Zero Trust assumes breach and demands continuous verification for every access attempt regardless of origin (Gartner, 2025). Key components relevant to foundations include network segmentation, role-based access controls, automated monitoring for anomalous behavior, and continuous authentication.

3. Regulatory Landscape: Expanding Obligations

3.1 State Privacy Laws

As of mid-2025, comprehensive data privacy laws are in force in 13 states, with three more taking effect by October 2025 (Wagenmaker & Oberly, 2025; VeraSafe, 2025).

The nonprofit exemption is narrowing. New Jersey’s SB 332 (effective January 2025) establishes broad privacy requirements with no nonprofit exemption. Oregon’s OCPA stripped nonprofits of exemptions after a one-year grace period. Minnesota’s Consumer Data Privacy Act (effective July 2025) includes nonprofits and provides expanded consumer rights (White & Case, 2025).

All 50 states have adopted data breach notification laws, and nonprofits are generally subject to these requirements regardless of sector-specific exemptions in other privacy laws.

3.2 Federal Regulatory Activity

The FTC has demonstrated willingness to enforce against organizations handling nonprofit data, as evidenced by the Blackbaud settlement. The FTC’s order required data deletion, security improvements, and ongoing compliance monitoring — setting a precedent for enforcement against vendors serving the philanthropic sector (FTC, May 2024).

3.3 Government Data Demands

An emerging risk for foundations is government-initiated demands for sensitive organizational data. In 2024, the Texas Attorney General issued a civil investigative demand for internal records and communications from PFLAG (Protect Democracy, 2024). Data integrity practices — including knowing exactly what data the organization holds, where it is stored, and what access controls govern it — become a matter of institutional protection.

4. Frameworks and Standards

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (released February 2024) is the most relevant standards framework for foundation cybersecurity. It organizes cybersecurity into six functions — Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover — and is explicitly designed to scale from small nonprofits to large enterprises (NIST, February 2024).

The addition of the Govern function in CSF 2.0 elevates cybersecurity from an IT concern to a board-level governance responsibility — addressing risk appetite, regulatory requirements, and accountability before technical investments are made. For foundations accustomed to board oversight of financial and programmatic risk, this framing maps naturally to existing governance structures.

5. Recommendations for Foundation Leadership

Immediate actions (0–90 days): Conduct a data inventory. Assess third-party vendor security. Implement multi-factor authentication across all systems handling donor or grantee data.

Near-term actions (90–180 days): Adopt the NIST CSF 2.0 Govern function as a framework for board-level cybersecurity oversight. Conduct an IP asset inventory. Develop an incident response plan specific to the foundation’s data types and regulatory obligations.

Strategic actions (6–18 months): Evaluate Zero Trust Architecture for remote and hybrid work. Build state privacy law compliance infrastructure proactively, regardless of current exemption status. Engage with sector-level cybersecurity initiatives including the CyberPeace Builders program and GCA’s Common Good Cyber Fund.

Research date: March 14, 2026. This research covers developments through early 2026; data privacy regulations and cybersecurity threat intelligence are among the most rapidly evolving domains.
