This is the companion piece to the workflow-integration stage of a Malaniru engagement. The earlier steps put governance and AI awareness in place; this is where the team learns to work inside a real, configured environment rather than a generic chat window. The summary below walks through the pieces of that environment — the application itself, the data policies that make it safe for sensitive work, the connectors that bring your existing tools into reach, and the skills and automation that turn one-off prompts into repeatable workflows.

Nothing here requires a technical background. The goal is for you to understand what each piece does and why it’s there, so the hands-on sessions build on a clear foundation.

The Environment

Why we work in the Claude desktop app

Most people meet AI through a browser tab: a single text box, one conversation at a time, no memory of what you did yesterday. That’s fine for quick questions. It is not a workspace.

The Malaniru environment runs in the Claude desktop application in what’s called “work in a folder” mode. Instead of a disconnected chat, Claude works directly inside a folder on your computer — reading the files that are there, writing new ones back, and keeping the structure organized. Your documents, templates, notes, and outputs all live in one place that you control, on your own machine. Claude becomes a colleague who can see the filing cabinet, not a stranger you have to describe everything to.

This matters for three practical reasons. First, context: the work persists. The environment remembers your projects, your preferences, and prior decisions across sessions, so you aren’t starting from zero each morning. Second, control: files stay in your folder, named and routed the way your work actually flows. Third, capability: the desktop app is where connectors, skills, and scheduled automation are available — the features that move you from “asking an AI questions” to “running your workflow with an AI inside it.”

For each Client, we build a trimmed-down version of this workspace — configured for your team’s actual tasks, without the parts you don’t need. You’re learning the environment you’ll use, not a demo of one.

Anthropic’s data and data-training policies

For work involving foundation and grantee data, where your information goes is not a footnote. It’s the first question, and it deserves a clear answer.

Anthropic — the company that makes Claude — handles data differently depending on which kind of plan you’re on, and the distinction is important:

Consumer plans (Claude Free, Pro, and Max). As of an August 2025 policy change, conversations on these plans can be used to help train future Claude models unless you turn that setting off. The control is a toggle labeled “Help improve Claude” in Privacy Settings. If it’s left on, data can be retained for up to five years; if you switch it off, the older 30-day deletion window applies and your chats are not used for training. New users choose during signup; existing users were prompted to make a selection.

Commercial plans (Claude for Work / Team, Enterprise, Education, Gov, and the API). These are excluded from the consumer training policy. Data from commercial accounts is not used to train Anthropic’s models by default, and there is no opt-in/opt-out toggle because the commercial terms already cover it.

The practical guidance for your work is straightforward: for anything touching sensitive foundation, donor, or grantee information, you want to be operating under terms where your data is not used for training. On a consumer plan that means confirming “Help improve Claude” is off; on a commercial plan that protection is the default.

Connectors

Bringing your existing tools into the workspace

A connector is a secure link between Claude and a service you already use. Once connected, Claude can work with the information in that service directly — find a document, read an email thread, check a task list — without you copying and pasting it back and forth. You authorize each connection, and you can see and revoke them.

The connectors configured for this environment cover the everyday tools a consultant actually lives in:

  • Google Drive — find, read, and reference documents, spreadsheets, and folders stored in Drive.

  • Gmail — search and read email threads, draft replies, and pull context out of correspondence.

  • Asana — look up tasks and projects, check what’s assigned and what’s due, and keep project tracking in sync with the work.

  • Box — access files and folders stored in Box, a common system of record for organizations with stricter document-management needs.

  • Office 365 — work with Microsoft documents, email, and calendar for teams that run on the Microsoft stack.

The point of connectors is to remove the busywork of moving information between tools. Instead of exporting a task list from Asana, pasting it into a chat, and asking for a summary, you ask once and Claude reaches the source itself. The same data-handling care applies here: a connector only does what you’ve authorized, and sensitive-data judgment still belongs to you.

The Skill Builder

Teaching the workspace your repeatable jobs

A skill is a saved, reusable instruction set — a way to package “how we do this particular task” so it runs the same way every time, without re-explaining it. Think of a skill as a recipe the workspace already knows.

The skill builder is the tool for creating and refining these. When you find yourself doing the same kind of work repeatedly — formatting a grant summary a specific way, drafting a standard client update, running a particular review checklist — that pattern can be turned into a skill. Once built, it’s available on demand and behaves consistently, which is exactly what you want when the output has to meet a standard rather than just “look about right.”

For the team, the value is durability: a good skill captures the right way to do something once, so the quality doesn’t depend on whoever happens to be at the keyboard or how well they remember the steps that day.

Open-Source Skills

Malaniru’s open-source skills

Beyond skills you build yourself, Malaniru maintains a set of open-source skills that come configured in the workspace. Three are worth knowing from day one:

Prompt Builder

Turns a rough request into a complete, well-structured instruction before any work begins. You start a message with Prompt: and describe what you need; the skill asks a few targeted clarifying questions, assembles a proper brief, and then runs it — routing the result to the right folder automatically. It’s the difference between firing off a vague ask and handing over a clear assignment. It also keeps an organized trail of what was produced and where, so the workspace stays tidy on its own.

Research

A structured research workflow that gathers information from reputable sources, checks claims rather than just asserting them, and produces a dated, cited write-up. It’s built around a simple discipline that runs through everything Malaniru does: a wrong answer is worse than no answer, so sources are required and uncertain claims are flagged rather than dressed up as fact. You trigger it by starting a message with Research: followed by the topic.

Project Memory

Keeps the thread across sessions and across projects. It captures the current state of a piece of work — open decisions, what’s done, what’s next, which files matter — into a project record, so when you return days later (or hand off to a colleague), the context is there waiting rather than locked in someone’s head. This is what lets the workspace feel continuous instead of resetting every time you open it.

These skills are open source, which means the way they work is inspectable and not a black box — a deliberate choice for an organization that has to be able to explain its tools to data-conscious clients.

Scheduled Tasks

Work that runs without you

The workspace can also do things on a schedule, without you starting them by hand. A scheduled task is a saved instruction set to run at a chosen time or on a repeating cadence — every morning, each Monday, the first of the month.

The useful pattern here is the recurring job you’d otherwise have to remember: a morning briefing assembled before you sit down, a weekly status digest, a standing check that flags anything needing attention. You set it up once; it runs on its own and the result is waiting for you. For irreversible or external actions — anything that sends, publishes, or deletes — the standing rule is that a human approves before it happens. Automation handles the preparation; people keep the final say.

The Whole Picture

How the pieces fit together

Taken individually, each of these is a feature. Taken together, they’re a way of working:

  • The desktop app in folder mode gives you a real workspace instead of a chat window.

  • Anthropic’s data terms, set correctly, keep sensitive work safe.

  • Connectors bring your existing tools — Drive, Gmail, Asana, Box, Office 365 — into that workspace.

  • The skill builder and Malaniru’s open-source skills turn repeated work into reliable, repeatable jobs.

  • Scheduled tasks let the routine parts run on their own, with humans in control of anything that matters.

The hands-on sessions are where this stops being a description and becomes muscle memory. You don’t need to master all of it at once — you need to understand the shape of the environment well enough to start using it deliberately. That’s what this summary is for.
