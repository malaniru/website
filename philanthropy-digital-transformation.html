Digital Transformation and How It Applies to Philanthropy

By Marco Harris·March 20, 2026

An evidence-based analysis of where the philanthropic sector stands on digital transformation, what is driving adoption, what is holding it back, and where the most meaningful opportunities exist for foundations.

← All philanthropy & AI research

Executive Summary

The philanthropic sector is in the early stages of a digital transformation that most other professional services industries began a decade ago. Fewer than 15 percent of nonprofits globally are considered digitally mature (Source: Salesforce, “Nonprofit Statistics and Trends,” 2022), and foundations — the funders themselves — are not materially ahead of the organizations they support. The 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, conducted by the Technology Association of Grantmakers (TAG) with over 350 participating organizations, found that 28 percent of grantmakers invest just 6–10 percent of their operating budgets in technology, down from 36 percent in 2022 (Source: TAG, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, October 2024). Technology budgets are shrinking even as the demands on foundation operations — compliance, reporting, remote collaboration, data governance — are expanding.

This report examines where the philanthropic sector stands on digital transformation, what is driving adoption, what is holding it back, and where the most meaningful opportunities exist for foundations willing to move beyond experimentation toward integrated digital infrastructure.

1. The Digital Maturity Gap in Philanthropy

The philanthropic sector’s digital maturity lags behind nearly every comparable professional services field. A Salesforce study found that only 12 percent of nonprofits have achieved “digital maturity,” even though 74 percent of nonprofit leaders agree that digital transformation is important (Source: Salesforce, “Nonprofit Statistics and Trends,” 2022). Digitally mature organizations are four times more likely to achieve their mission goals and twice as likely to see improvements in operational efficiency.

For foundations specifically, the picture is similarly constrained. The 2022 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey found that half of foundations allocate 5 percent or less of their operating budgets to technology (Source: TAG / PEAK Grantmaking, 2022 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey). The most common technology stack at a small to mid-sized private foundation remains a patchwork: a grants management system (if one exists at all), email, shared drives, and spreadsheets. Integrated digital workflows — where data flows from grant application through review, award, monitoring, and reporting without manual intervention — remain the exception.

This gap has consequences. Foundations running on manual processes face longer grant cycles, inconsistent reporting, higher administrative burden per grant dollar, and limited ability to use their own data for strategic decision-making. The opportunity cost is not abstract: every hour a program officer spends reformatting a grantee report or reconciling spreadsheets is an hour not spent on mission-driven work.

2. What Is Driving Digital Transformation in Philanthropy

Several forces are converging to push foundations toward digital modernization, even in a sector that has historically been slow to adopt new technology.

Funder and Regulatory Reporting Requirements

Since approximately 2022, major foundations and government funders have moved toward requiring digital reporting portals and standardized outcome data (Source: CflowApps, “Nonprofit Digital Transformation in 2026,” 2026). Organizations without robust digital tools risk losing access to competitive funding opportunities. IRS 990-PF reporting, expenditure responsibility documentation, and state charity registration all generate compliance burdens that manual processes handle poorly at scale.

Remote and Hybrid Work as a Permanent Shift

The TAG 2024 survey found that 69 percent of grantmakers plan to remain in or move to a hybrid/remote workplace model (Source: TAG, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, October 2024). Remote work demands digital-first infrastructure: cloud-based document management, collaborative platforms, and secure remote access to grants management systems.

Donor and Stakeholder Expectations

Donors in 2026 increasingly expect organizations to demonstrate modern digital practices: functional websites, current CRM systems, mobile-friendly interfaces, and transparent reporting (Source: Foundation Source, “2026 Giving Outlook,” January 2026). Younger donors entering the philanthropic space favor mobile giving tools, online grantmaking platforms, and digital-first communication (Source: Dorothy A. Johnson Center for Philanthropy, “11 Trends in Philanthropy for 2026,” January 2026).

The AI Catalyst

AI adoption has accelerated the urgency of digital transformation by exposing a prerequisite that many foundations have not met: you cannot implement AI effectively on top of fragmented, manual infrastructure. The 81 percent of foundations reporting some AI usage (Source: TAG, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, October 2024) are discovering that experimentation with AI in isolation — without underlying digital infrastructure — produces limited returns.

3. Where Foundations Are Today: The Technology Landscape

Grants Management Systems

The grants management system (GMS) market for foundations has consolidated around a handful of platforms: Fluxx, Submittable, Foundant GLM, SmartSimple, and GivingData are the most widely adopted (Source: PEAK Grantmaking / Grantbook / TAG / Tech Impact, “A Consumer’s Guide to Grants Management Systems,” 6th Edition). A dominant theme in survey responses: grantmakers want platforms that are more intuitive and user-friendly, reducing complexity for both funders and grantees.

Foundations implementing comprehensive grant management solutions have reported reducing administrative time by up to 70 percent (Self-reported — independent verification unavailable; Source: TAG, referenced by Optimy, “Effective Grant Solutions,” 2025).

Data Infrastructure

Candid, formed from the 2019 merger of GuideStar and Foundation Center, represents the sector’s most significant data infrastructure initiative. Their unified search platform integrates nonprofit and funder data, enabling more efficient matching between grant seekers and grantmakers (Source: Candid, “Launch of Candid Search,” 2024).

For individual foundations, data infrastructure typically means a CRM, a GMS, and whatever reporting tools are available within those platforms. The TAG 2024 survey found that only 45 percent of grantmakers have a data privacy policy and only 46 percent have a data retention and destruction policy (Source: TAG, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, October 2024).

AI Adoption: Wide Experimentation, Narrow Integration

While 81 percent of foundations report some AI usage, enterprise-wide adoption is emergent: just 4 percent indicate AI usage across the entire organization (Source: TAG, 2024 State of Philanthropy Tech Survey, October 2024). The primary barriers are privacy and security concerns (55 percent), lack of necessary skills (43 percent), and uncertainty about relevant use cases (40 percent).

The governance gap is notable: only 30 percent of foundations have an AI policy in place, and just 9 percent have both an AI policy and an AI advisory committee. Community foundations lead on governance, with 33 percent having implemented AI policies.

The Center for Effective Philanthropy (CEP) has identified a critical inflection point: the sector needs to transition from experimenting with AI to developing a strategy for operational effectiveness, impact innovation, and nonprofit support (Source: CEP, “An AI Roadmap for Philanthropy in 2025,” 2025).

4. The Practical Dimensions of Digital Transformation for Foundations

Digital transformation in philanthropy is not a single technology purchase. It is a phased modernization across four dimensions.

Workflow Digitization. Moving from paper-based and email-driven processes to structured digital workflows. For many small foundations (3–15 staff), this is the most impactful intervention — it reduces the administrative tax on lean teams and creates the data foundation for everything that follows.

Data Integration and Governance. Ensuring that data flows between systems rather than being trapped in silos. This requires data governance basics — data privacy policies, retention policies, access controls — that fewer than half of foundations currently have in place.

Analytics and Decision Support. Using the data generated by digital workflows to inform strategic decisions. Which program areas produce the strongest grantee outcomes? Where does the grant cycle bottleneck? Most foundations cannot answer these questions today because their data is fragmented.

Intelligent Automation. Applying AI and automation to reduce repetitive tasks and augment staff capacity. Document drafting, grant application screening, report synthesis, board materials preparation — these are the use cases where AI can deliver measurable time-for-mission returns. But this layer depends on the three below it being reasonably mature.

5. Barriers and Risks

Budget Constraints. Technology budgets are declining as a percentage of operating expenses at exactly the moment when digital investment is most needed.

Staffing and Skills Gaps. Forty-three percent of foundations cite lack of necessary skills as a barrier. Small foundations rarely have dedicated technology staff.

Cultural Resistance. Philanthropy has a mission culture that can be skeptical of technology investment. The framing matters: digital transformation is about capacity augmentation that returns time to mission-driven work.

Vendor Dependency. The foundation technology market is concentrated. A small number of GMS providers serve the majority of the market, and switching costs are high.

6. Opportunities and Recommendations

For foundations beginning their digital journey: start with workflow digitization and data governance basics. Implement a modern GMS if one is not in place. Establish data privacy and retention policies.

For foundations with existing digital infrastructure: focus on integration and governance. Establish an AI policy and, if resources permit, an AI advisory committee. The 9 percent of foundations that have both are positioned to move from experimentation to strategic deployment.

For the sector as a whole: treat digital infrastructure as infrastructure — not as a program expense. The operational efficiency gains from digital maturity are not marginal; they are the difference between foundations that can scale to meet the moment and those that cannot.
