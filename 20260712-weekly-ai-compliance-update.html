Executive Summary

The compliance calendar tightened this week rather than shifting. The EU AI Act's 2 August 2026 milestone is now three weeks out, and it carries two things at once: the Article 50 transparency obligations begin to apply, and the European Commission gains the power to fine general-purpose AI (GPAI) model providers — up to 3% of worldwide annual turnover or €15 million, whichever is higher (✅ Verified). The "Digital Omnibus" that reshuffled the high-risk deadlines still had not been published in the Official Journal as of this writing, and formal publication is widely anticipated in July, before the August date (⚠️ Uncertain on the exact day). In the United States, the near-term action is administrative: Colorado's Attorney General is collecting pre-rulemaking public comment on the state's replacement AI law through 13 July, and the FTC opened a comment window on a proposed AI-accuracy policy statement while settling an algorithmic tenant-screening case for $2.25 million (✅ Verified). On the litigation front, the New York Times asked a federal court to sanction OpenAI over disputed evidence handling in the ChatGPT-logs discovery fight (✅ Verified). None of this is a checklist; several items are still moving.

2. New Laws & Rulemaking

Colorado — AG pre-rulemaking comment window closing (this week). The Colorado Attorney General's office is in the pre-rulemaking phase for SB 26-189, the law that repealed and reenacted the state's AI Act, and is collecting informal public input through a comment form open until 13 July 2026 (✅ Verified). The rulemaking is expected to clarify some of the law's least-settled points, including the meaning of "materially influence" and the mechanics of post-adverse-outcome notices, with final rules due on or before 1 January 2027 (✅ Verified). Until those rules issue, several operative definitions remain open (⚠️ Uncertain). What this means for you: organizations that will be deployers or developers under the Colorado law have a live, if brief, channel to shape how key terms get defined.

EU — Digital Omnibus awaiting Official Journal publication. The AI Act simplification package that defers the high-risk deadlines (stand-alone Annex III systems to 2 December 2027; AI embedded in Annex I products to 2 August 2028) still takes legal effect only on publication in the Official Journal, which multiple firm analyses expect in July 2026 ahead of the August date (✅ Verified that publication is pending; ⚠️ Uncertain on the exact date). The 2 August 2026 Article 50 transparency layer is not deferred (✅ Verified). What this means for you: the deferral many teams are counting on is not formally in force until publication lands, so plans that assume the later deadlines rest on an expected-but-not-yet-final step.

Connecticut — SB 5's ADMT provisions on the horizon. Beyond the privacy amendments that took effect 1 July, Connecticut's SB 5 phases in from 1 October 2026 through 1 October 2027 and includes disclosure and accountability duties where AI "materially influences" consequential employment decisions (✅ Verified). It joins Colorado and California in the growing cluster of state automated-decision regimes with staggered start dates.

3. Enforcement & Official Guidance

FTC — proposed AI-accuracy policy statement out for comment. The FTC issued a proposed policy statement on AI accuracy and "output steering," with public comments due 31 July 2026 (✅ Verified). The statement's premise is that when a company represents its AI system as accurate or objective, deception principles can apply if the system is designed to pursue undisclosed objectives — the proposal names examples such as altering outputs for ideological ends, in response to political pressure, or to dodge state-law liability (✅ Verified). This is a proposal in a comment period, not a final rule (⚠️ Uncertain as to final form). What this means for you: representations about an AI feature's accuracy or neutrality are the kind of marketing claim consumer-protection regulators are signaling they will read closely.

FTC — $2.25M tenant-screening settlement. On 9 July 2026, RentGrow agreed to pay $2.25 million to resolve FTC allegations under the Fair Credit Reporting Act and the FTC Act tied to its tenant-screening practices (✅ Verified). The action is a reminder that algorithmic scoring used in consequential decisions sits squarely inside existing consumer-protection and fair-credit law, independent of any AI-specific statute (🔶 Inferred as to the broader read).

NYT v. OpenAI — sanctions request over disputed evidence (this week; correction). On 9 July 2026, the New York Times and co-plaintiffs asked the court to discipline OpenAI, contending it withheld or mishandled evidence and asking that OpenAI be barred from relying on the 20-million-log sample as unreliable (✅ Verified). Correction to last week's issue: the order compelling production of the roughly 20-million ChatGPT-log sample was affirmed by Judge Sidney Stein earlier in the litigation — reputable reporting places that affirmation in January 2026, not late June — and the docket has continued to generate rulings since; the exact procedural chronology is best treated as ⚠️ Uncertain and confirmed against the docket. The through-line is unchanged: user-conversation data entered into consumer AI tools can be pulled into litigation the user is not party to. What this means for you: many organizations keep regulated data on enterprise or API tiers with contractual data terms precisely to reduce this exposure.

Federal preemption pressure — still live, no court resolution. The December 2025 executive order and its DOJ AI Litigation Task Force continue to shape the state landscape rather than settle it; the order expressly carves child-safety and certain other state laws out of its preemption push, and reporting notes 2026 state activity has tilted toward child safety, data centers, and consumer protection (✅ Verified). No definitive court ruling resolving the federal-versus-state preemption question is reported as of this issue (⚠️ Uncertain).

4. Vendor Policy & Data-Handling

Anthropic — ID/biometric verification now in effect (continuity update). The government-ID and facial-verification requirement for some consumer Claude accounts, flagged last week as upcoming, took effect 8 July 2026 (✅ Verified). As previously noted, verification runs through a third-party identity provider, and the reporting does not publish a specific retention period for the verification data (⚠️ Uncertain); the change is described as applying to consumer tiers rather than enterprise or API access. Facial-geometry data can qualify as biometric data under laws such as Illinois's BIPA and several state privacy statutes, so who collects and holds it remains the relevant question.

Retention posture as a procurement variable. Reporting this week continued to frame model-vendor retention and deletion terms — including zero-data-retention configurations and the divergence between consumer defaults and enterprise/commercial terms — as a driver of AI procurement decisions rather than a footnote (✅ Verified as reporting/analysis). The practical point is consistent with prior weeks: the data-handling posture of a tool an organization already uses can change on a vendor's timetable, independent of any statute, so access-governance, retention, and identity-verification terms are worth reviewing at the tier level.

The four sections rhyme this week: most of the movement is procedural — comment windows, an awaited Official Journal date, a sanctions motion — rather than newly binding obligations. That is the normal texture of a tightening calendar, and it is where watching beats reacting.
